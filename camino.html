What is the Camino?

The Camino de Santiago (the "Way of St. James") is a network of ancient pilgrimage routes across Europe that all lead to one place: the cathedral in Santiago de Compostela, Spain, where tradition holds that the remains of the apostle James are buried.

People have walked these paths for over a thousand years — originally as an act of religious pilgrimage, and today for reasons that range from deep faith to grief, searching, a personal challenge, or simple curiosity. Tens of thousands of people still walk it every year, some for a few days and others for over a month.

Evangelism on the Camino

The most walked of all the routes is the Camino Francés, stretching roughly 500 miles from St. Jean Pied de Port in France, across northern Spain, to Santiago de Compostela. It typically takes 30–35 days to walk in full.

Caldas de Reis, where we'll be living, sits along the final stretch of this route — one of the last towns pilgrims pass through, just a couple of days' walk from the cathedral itself.

Why it matters for this ministry

Walking that many miles, day after day, tends to strip away distraction. Pilgrims often arrive at conversations — with themselves, with each other, with God — that wouldn't happen in ordinary life. Many are walking through grief, a major life transition, or a genuine search for meaning.

That's exactly why a rest stop along the way, run by people who genuinely want to listen and offer hospitality, can mean so much. It's not a sales pitch — it's a bench, a cup of coffee, and a willingness to sit with someone in whatever they're carrying.
